# Interactions and assessment

## Interaction must serve a learning verb

Every interaction should make the learner do at least one of:

- predict
- retrieve
- classify
- compare
- manipulate
- construct
- explain
- debug
- apply
- transfer
- reflect

A control that only animates decoration is not a learning interaction.

## Feedback timing

Give immediate feedback for routine practice. Delay final answers when a second
attempt is likely to produce useful thinking. Preserve the original response
before reveal or revision.

## Hint ladders

Hints should increase in specificity:

1. orient attention
2. recall a relevant idea
3. narrow the choice or next step
4. model part of the process
5. explain directly

Record which hints were opened. After direct explanation, require a fresh
application or reconstruction.

## Question design

- Avoid answer-length and formatting clues.
- Make distractors reflect plausible misconceptions.
- Ask for reasoning when it is the target, not for every trivial item.
- Separate confidence from correctness.
- Include at least one changed-context task when transfer matters.
- Avoid self-assessment as the only evidence.

## Return data

Useful evidence includes:

- original and revised answers
- attempts
- hint IDs used
- confidence before and after
- correctness where deterministically known
- self-explanation
- simulation parameters or meaningful states explored
- skipped activities
- anchored notes and questions
- perceived difficulty and helpfulness

Collect the minimum useful evidence. Avoid surveillance metrics with unclear
learning value.
