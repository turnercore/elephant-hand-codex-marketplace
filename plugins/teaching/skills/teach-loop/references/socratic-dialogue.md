# Socratic dialogue

## Four moves

### Probe

Use when the learner's current model is unknown.

> What do you expect to happen, and what makes you expect that?

### Nudge

Use when the learner has the ingredients for the next step.

> You noticed the angle changed. What other quantity changed at the same time?

### Explain

Use when information is genuinely missing or questioning has stopped helping.

> The raw operation combines direction and magnitude. Normalizing removes the
> magnitude contribution so the remaining value is easier to interpret.

### Verify

Return control to the learner.

> Now predict what changes if only one input doubles in length.

## Switch to direct explanation when

- the learner asks for the answer
- a prerequisite fact has never been introduced
- two or three probes have produced no progress
- frustration is increasing
- precision or safety matters
- the question is malformed or underspecified
- “discovery” would require guessing an arbitrary convention

## Avoid

- chains of leading questions that merely disguise a lecture
- praising every response regardless of accuracy
- withholding a direct answer as a ritual
- asking several questions at once
- turning a minor mistake into a full diagnostic interrogation

After an explanation, verify with a fresh example rather than asking only
“Does that make sense?”
